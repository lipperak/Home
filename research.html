Hydro-chemical interaction between active particles


In recent years, self-propelled catalytic particles have attracted much attention as biomimetic alternatives to biological microorganisms, in order to study individual and collective dynamics at the micron scale. They are also potential candidates to perform controlled applications in the field of biomedical engineering (e.g. targeted drug delivery or microsurgery) or to actively control the macroscopic behavior of a synthetic suspension.
Generically, these systems rely on a chemical activity of their surface (namely the ability to change the physico-chemical properties of their environment such as temperature orsolute content) and surface mobility that generate a surface slip velocity or stress forcing in response to local thermodynamic gradients (e.g. phoretic mobility or Marangoni stress). Examples of such phoretic systems are bi-metallic catalytic swimmers or reactive droplets, that release a solute in their environment.
Most of these systems are not neutrally-buoyant and evolve next to a boundary (i.e. wall or free surface). Yet most of the existing models focus on individual and collective modeling in bulk (i.e. far from these surfaces). This project will therefore focus on the role of confining boundaries in setting individual particles' locomotion properties as well as their interactions


Active droplets swim in a viscous fluid thanks to the Marangoni flows forced at their surface by self-generated gradients of a chemical solute produced by the droplets. Each swimming droplet modifies the solute content and flow field seen by its neighbours. This project studies the competition of these two routes of interaction, as well as the exact interaction of active droplets with confining boundaries

Propulsion of micro particles in low-Reynolds flows

In order to propel in a Stokes flow, a micro-particle has to break somewhere a symmetry. We could think about E.Coli bacteria whose helicoïdal flagellum enables it to perform non revertible in time motions and leads to its propulsion.
In this project we focus on the impact of slight deviation from a homogeneous sphere that evolves in low-Reynolds (but non zero) flows.

Project in collaboration with Michael Benzaquen, Olivier Dauchot and Sebastien Michelin.

Wetting at the nano-scale

This project in collaboration with Caroline Mortagne, Michael Benzaquen and Thierry Ondarçuhu aims to study the physics of fuid-structure interactions at the nanoscale in order to better understand the wetting phenomenon at the macro one.

Social Dynamics

This project in collaboration with David Martin aims to study the dynamical organization of groups of individuals especially self-arrangement and utility dynamics regarding passengers in public transports.
